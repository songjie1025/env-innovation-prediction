## One-Paragraph Summary

Brunnermeier and Cohen (2003) studies determinants of environmental innovation in US manufacturing industries. Subagent screening reports that it examines 146 US manufacturing industries from 1983-1992, using successful environmental patent applications assigned to industries and predictors such as pollution-abatement expenditures, regulatory monitoring, shipments, concentration, capital intensity, and export intensity. The main accessible finding is that pollution-abatement expenditures are associated with more environmental patents, while monitoring or enforcement visits do not add a clear innovation incentive. The unit of analysis is not country-year, so it should not dominate the project design, but it provides supporting evidence for regulatory-cost pressure and industrial-structure mechanisms. Publisher access may be restricted, so full details should be verified before `Notes reviewed`.

## Key Findings for Predictor Selection

1. Pollution-abatement expenditures are positively associated with environmental patents, supporting regulatory or compliance-cost pressure.
2. Monitoring/enforcement visits do not clearly add innovation incentives, complicating a simple "more regulation means more patents" story.
3. Export intensity or international competition may matter, supporting industrial structure as a rough control rather than a core country predictor.

## Strengths

1. More directly focused on environmental innovation than broad patent-output studies.
2. Separates abatement-cost pressure from monitoring/enforcement.
3. Published in a strong environmental economics journal.

## Limitations

1. Data coverage limitations: US manufacturing only.
2. Measurement limitations: Industry-level environmental patents do not map exactly to OECD country-year patent shares.
3. Identification or interpretation limitations: Regulation and industry structure may be endogenous.
4. Transferability limitations: Country-level WDI proxies are much coarser than the original industry measures.

## How We Should Use This Source

`Supporting`: Use this as evidence that regulatory or compliance-cost pressure can be associated with environmental patenting, and as a caution that not every regulatory measure has the same innovation signal. Do not use it as central support for macro, R&D, or energy-system predictors.

## Extractable Text for Report

1. Industry-level evidence suggests that environmental innovation is linked to both regulatory pressure and innovation capacity.
2. A country-level manufacturing share can be interpreted as a rough structural control, but it is weaker evidence than R&D or EPS.

## Open Questions After Reading

1. What exact environmental patent classes and years does the paper use?
2. Are the reported effects robust enough to justify manufacturing share in a small country-year model?
