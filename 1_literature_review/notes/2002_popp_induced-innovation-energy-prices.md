## One-Paragraph Summary

Popp (2002) is a canonical induced-innovation paper showing that changes in energy prices can redirect inventive activity toward energy-saving technologies. Subagent screening reports that the paper uses US energy patent data from 1970-1994, with regressions mainly through 1991, and combines energy prices, technology-specific citation-weighted knowledge stocks, federal energy R&D, and selected technology controls. It is not a country-year environmental patent paper in the same form as this project, but it is high-impact and conceptually important because it explains why economic pressure from the energy system can shape innovation. For our predictor set, the paper supports the idea that energy-related incentives can predict future patenting, especially with lags and knowledge-stock dynamics. However, it also creates a warning: WDI energy intensity or fossil energy share are not the same as energy prices.

## Key Findings for Predictor Selection

1. Energy prices positively predict energy-saving patents, supporting energy-cost pressure more than energy-consumption shares.
2. Citation-weighted knowledge stocks matter, so innovation capacity and lagged inventive history are conceptually important.
3. Federal energy R&D has period-dependent or smaller effects than a simple R&D story would suggest.
4. Energy-system proxies in our project need cautious interpretation because they are not direct energy-price variables.

## Strengths

1. High-impact, canonical source for induced innovation.
2. Provides a clear mechanism for lagged energy-related predictors.
3. Helps distinguish direct incentives from weak structural proxies.

## Limitations

1. Data coverage limitations: Not a direct country-year OECD panel source.
2. Measurement limitations: Uses energy prices and knowledge stocks, not the project's WDI energy variables.
3. Identification or interpretation limitations: Mechanism does not automatically validate fossil share, energy intensity, or CO2 as predictors.
4. Transferability limitations: Historical and country context may differ from the project's global panel.

## How We Should Use This Source

`Core`: Use this as the main conceptual source for induced innovation and energy-pressure mechanisms. It should make the report more careful: if energy intensity or fossil share enters the model, describe it as a proxy with ambiguous sign, not as the same evidence as energy prices.
